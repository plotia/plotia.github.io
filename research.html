Since Fall 2024, I have been conducting molecular dynamics simulations of the wild-type Triose Phosphate Isomerase (TPI) enzyme at the Koes Group, using the AMBER software suite. TPI is an essential enzyme that catalyzes the reversible interconversion of dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and D-glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P) during glycolysis, a key metabolic pathway for energy production.

A mutation at position E104D within TPI does not eliminate enzymatic activity, but it severely reduces the enzyme's thermal stability. This destabilization leads to increased degradation rates of the protein and a marked reduction in its catalytic efficiency. Over time, this can cause severe metabolic imbalances in patients with this mutation.

There are two primary biochemical consequences of the E104D mutation, which can lead to a patient's death within five years of the mutation's onset. The first reason is that TPI’s catalytic activity is crucial for glycolysis. TPI converts DHAP into G3P, which is further metabolized to pyruvate and ultimately to ATP, the cell’s primary energy source. In patients with the E104D mutation, DHAP accumulates as its conversion to G3P is impaired.

This accumulation of DHAP has a detrimental side effect: it inhibits the enzyme myo-inositol phosphate synthase, a key enzyme in the inositol biosynthesis pathway. Myo-inositol is a precursor for phosphatidylinositol, which plays a role in various signaling pathways, including membrane dynamics and stress responses. The inhibition of this pathway exacerbates the symptoms of TPI deficiency, contributing to systemic energy deficits and cellular dysfunction, eventually leading to neurodegeneration and other life-threatening complications.

Thus, while the E104D mutation does not directly eliminate TPI's function, its destabilizing effects on the enzyme lead to a cascade of metabolic disruptions that have profound impacts on cellular homeostasis and overall patient health.

Skills: Molecular Dynamics, Amber, Python, bash, ssh, MDAnalysis, py3dmol, VMD, openmm, Amoeba

Niepa μ biointerface Lab :

Before joining the Koes Group, I spent the summer working at the Niepa Microbiointerface Lab, where I focused on investigating the rheological properties of microbes, particularly biofilms formed by ESKAPEE pathogens such as E. coli, P. aeruginosa, and S. aureus. My goal in this lab was to gain hands-on experience in wet lab techniques for cell culture cultivation and validate a hypothesis I had previously proposed in my publication, Ayurvedic Remedies for Nosocomial Bacterial Infections. In that paper, we suggested that efflux pump inhibitors play a role in biofilm formation.

To test this, I collected baseline data using a rheometer, measuring shear stress versus shear rate for untreated E. coli, P. aeruginosa, and S. aureus cells. Additionally, I cultivated biofilms at the oil-water interface and gathered data using a tensiometer. Following this, I treated the cells with efflux pump inhibitors such as NMP, PAβN, and thioridazine, applying a concentration gradient on a 96-well plate. The cells were then incubated to allow for biofilm formation. After two days, I treated the cells with the appropriate antibiotics. A successful outcome would have been a measurable change in cell count across the efflux pump inhibitor gradient, indicating the inhibitors' impact on biofilm disruption.

Skills: Rheometry, Tensiometry, Bench-Top Spectrophotometer, Cell Culture

The Ly Lab :

The Danith Ly Lab at Carnegie Mellon University is known for its innovative research in chemical biology, particularly in the synthesis of nucleic acid-based molecules like peptide nucleic acids (PNAs). The lab’s work spans gene editing, molecular diagnostics, and the development of novel therapeutics, combining chemistry and biology to address critical challenges in medicine.
